## Introduction

This question is not new. Hobbes asked it, Marx asked it, and contemporary political philosophy continues to ask it in different forms. This paper does not respond to any of them directly. It offers a different entry point: what if the stability of a system does not depend on fairness at all, but on something more basic and more structural? The framework used here is drawn from the *Limit Theory of Meaning* — a theory developed independently and outlined elsewhere in this repository. The present paper simply extends it to the institutional level.

## The Signal Distribution System

### Definition of Signal

In this paper, "signal" refers to the survival resources, social status, security opportunities, and political participation space that flow within a system. Signals are not uniformly distributed; they are allocated according to the position, function, and exchange capacity of nodes. The stability of a system depends on the continuity of signal flow, not on the balance of signal distribution.

This paper primarily deals with resource signals—that is, actually allocated survival resources, security, and opportunities. Commitment signals (such as slogans, narratives) appear only as secondary references when relevant to legitimacy.

Signals have three elements: density, direction, and filtration. Density refers to the total resources and security a node receives; direction refers to signal flow from internal nodes to marginal nodes, while signals from marginal nodes are filtered out; filtration refers to the mechanisms by which the system suppresses signals from marginal nodes through institutional, cultural, or discursive means.

### Two Types of Nodes

Internal nodes are at the system's core—high signal density, comprehensive security, prioritized resource allocation, and relatively stable operation. Marginal nodes are at the system's periphery—low signal density, limited or near-zero security, and relatively vulnerable operation. The stability of the system does not require equal signal density between the two types of nodes; it only requires that signal flow is not interrupted.

### Definition of Stability

"Stability" in this paper refers to a state of operation in which the system has not experienced structural collapse. It does not imply "efficient operation" or "good performance." A system can remain stable under conditions of low efficiency and severe unfairness—as long as its core operational structure remains intact.


## The Mechanism of Exchange Continuity

### Definition of Exchange

"Exchange" in this paper refers to quantifiable transactional activities through which nodes obtain survival resources, including labor, consumption, and taxation. It does not include purely physical survival behaviors (such as breathing, sleeping), nor does it include symbolic behaviors that do not involve resource flow.

### Definition of Continuity

"Continuity" refers to the uninterrupted flow of overall signals to a degree that does not reach zeroing. It allows for local fluctuations and temporary exits. A system can continue to operate even if some nodes temporarily withdraw from exchange, as long as the overall signal flow has not been interrupted to a degree that makes the system unsustainable.

### Why Marginal Nodes Continue to Participate in Exchange

Even with extremely low signal density, marginal nodes are still compelled to participate in labor, consumption, investment, and taxation. This compelled participation is not based on identification with the system, but on the basic conditions of survival. Thus, even when the signal distribution structure heavily favors internal nodes, the exchange behaviors of marginal nodes continue to provide labor and consumer markets for the system, maintaining the fuel for signal flow.

### The Feedback Asymmetry of Private Property

The system's feedback loops primarily receive signals from internal nodes, while signals from marginal nodes are filtered or ignored. When internal nodes perceive a threat to their own signal density, the system quickly adjusts to maintain their operational stability. When marginal nodes send signals (such as poverty, unemployment, discontent), the system does not immediately make structural adjustments, but instead reduces signal intensity through local appeasement, discursive guidance, or institutional filtration. This feedback asymmetry allows the system to maintain stability even under conditions of severe unfairness.


## State Institutions as Signal Stabilizers

### The Dual Function of State Institutions

State institutions serve a dual function in the signal distribution system: as signal stabilizers, maintaining the continuity of signal flow through resource allocation, social security, and policy regulation; and as legitimacy maintainers, sustaining the trust expectations of marginal nodes toward the system through the release of commitment signals.

### The Coexistence of Full Coverage and Signal Zeroing

In many systems, full coverage (for internal nodes) coexists with signal zeroing (for marginal nodes) over long periods. This structure can persist because the survival conditions of marginal nodes are compressed to the minimum, yet they still participate in exchange. As long as this participation is not interrupted, the system will not collapse.

### Signal Distribution Differences Between Two Institutional Forms

Signal distribution structures differ across institutional forms, but the core logic of private property—maintaining signal flow through exchange—remains consistent across forms: in Form A (biased toward internal priority), signals flow preferentially to internal nodes, and marginal node signal density approaches zero over the long term; in Form B (biased toward market regulation), signals flow through exchange, and marginal node signal density is unstable but not completely zero. Both forms operate in the same direction, differing only in the proportion and speed of signal distribution.


## The Continuity Logic of Private Property

### Exchange, Not Fairness

The core operational logic of private property is exchange. Nodes participate in exchange through labor, consumption, taxation, investment, and other means, maintaining signal flow. Even if the signal density of marginal nodes is compressed to a very low level, as long as they continue to participate in exchange, the system can obtain the signals needed for its continuation from their participation—without needing to change their level of signal allocation.

### Unfairness as Part of Operational State

Unfairness is not a "fault signal" of the system; it is one of its operational states. Whether the system chooses to release adjustment signals at the boundary depends on whether its continuation requires such adjustment, not on the degree of unfairness.


## Structural Flaws of the "Replacement" Narrative

### Marx's "Inevitable Replacement"

Marx's claim that "communism will inevitably replace capitalism" is a historical prediction, not a signal structure analysis. It overlooks the system's capacity to release adjustment signals at the boundary to maintain its own continuation.

### The Buffering Effect of Adjustment Signals

The adjustment signals released by systems at the boundary—welfare states, labor rights, social security, tax regulation—do not change the core structure of private property, but they are sufficient to reduce the risks posed by signal rupture in marginal nodes. These adjustments allow the system to continue for a longer period without changing its core structure.


## The Relationship Between System Stability and Fairness

### The Source of Stability

System stability does not depend on fairness, but on the continuity of node participation. As long as the exchange network is not interrupted, the system can maintain operation. This explains why fairness issues at the institutional level can persist over the long term while the system still maintains its basic operational structure.

### The Levels of Fairness Rupture

Fairness rupture manifests as the structural differentiation between full coverage and signal zeroing. This differentiation can persist over the long term because it does not directly affect the continuity of exchange relations. The system can tolerate widespread signal distribution inequality, as long as such inequality does not threaten the basic structure of node participation in exchange.

### Why the System Does Not Need to Resolve Unfairness

Fairness is not the operational goal of private property. It is a secondary signal, and its density is insufficient to force the system to make structural adjustments. The system only releases local adjustment signals at the boundary to maintain the minimum level of node participation. This feature explains the resilience of private property systems in the face of long-term unfairness—they adjust, but they do not change their core structure.


## Testability and Falsifiability Conditions

Any testable theory must specify the conditions under which it would be falsified. The thesis of this paper—"system stability does not depend on fairness, but on the continuity of exchange relations"—has the following testable conditions:

### Falsifiability Condition 1 (Continuity Disruption)

If a private property system, after its marginal nodes exit exchange on a large scale (e.g., long-term mass unemployment, sharp decline in labor force participation), still maintains its original signal density and operational stability, then the thesis "system depends on exchange continuity" would no longer hold.

### Falsifiability Condition 2 (Structural Transformation)

If a private property system undergoes a complete structural transformation at the boundary (e.g., private property is fully replaced by public ownership), and this transformation is not achieved within the boundary adjustment range of private property, then the thesis "the system only undergoes morphological adjustment" would no longer hold.

### Falsifiability Condition 3 (Stable Operation of Non-Exchange Systems)

If there exists a system that does not depend on exchange relations between nodes yet can maintain stable signal flow over the long term, then the thesis "the stability of private property depends on exchange relations" would no longer hold.

### Operationality of Falsifiability Conditions

The observation window for the above conditions is set as "exit lasting more than 1/3 of the system's operating cycle without adjustment." To date, no cases satisfying these falsifiability conditions have been observed. The proposition has not been falsified within the current observational range.


## Objections and Responses

### Objection 1: Marginal Nodes Do Not Voluntarily Participate in Exchange

**Objection:** Marginal nodes do not "voluntarily" participate in exchange; they are compelled to do so because non-participation would make survival impossible. They are **forced** to remain in the system. The stability of the system is not due to "exchange" itself, but to the absence of alternatives.

**Response:** This objection reveals the distinction between "compelled participation" and "voluntary participation," but it does not negate the core thesis of this paper—it only reveals the **constraint conditions of exchange continuity**. The system does not require marginal nodes to participate "voluntarily"; it only requires them to participate "actually." Marginal nodes do not exit not because the system provides sufficient incentives, but because exit would mean a sharp rise in survival costs. This "exit cost" itself is the structural force maintaining exchange continuity.

**Supplementary Note:** "Not exiting" is not equivalent to "effective participation." When the participation of marginal nodes declines to a level that only sustains their own survival, the signal density they provide to the system decreases significantly. The system does not require every node to generate high-density signals, but it needs overall signal density to remain above a threshold.

**Case:** Approximately 40 million people in the United States rely on food assistance (SNAP) to maintain basic living standards. Many low-income households face a "cliff effect"—a slight increase in income may lead to the loss of all benefits, while the new income is far from enough to compensate for the loss. This "excessive exit cost" structure means that even if marginal nodes have the opportunity to improve their situation, they often choose to remain in the system's exchange relations.

### Objection 2: Unfairness Reduces System Efficiency

**Objection:** Long-term unfairness reduces production efficiency, increases social costs, hinders technological progress, and leads to deteriorating health among marginal groups. The system may still run, but it runs increasingly slowly, and may eventually be replaced due to low efficiency.

**Response:** This objection points to the **long-term costs** of fairness rupture, but it conflates "the system is still running" with "the system is running well." The thesis of this paper is that the system can maintain operation under conditions of unfairness—it does not promise that the system runs "well" or "efficiently," only that it "does not collapse." Unfairness does reduce system efficiency and increase social costs, but these costs accumulate gradually rather than exploding all at once.

**Supplementary Note:** The critical point of efficiency decline is not a fixed value. When social operating costs continue to rise and the system cannot offset them through adjustment signals, the system enters a cycle of "efficiency decline—adjustment space shrinking—further efficiency decline." The resilience of the system lies not in avoiding efficiency decline, but in whether it can complete morphological adjustment before efficiency decline reaches the critical point.

**Case:** After Russia's "shock therapy," industrial production fell to 58% of its 1989 level, and inflation rates exceeded 1000% for several consecutive years. But the system did not collapse. After Putin came to power, he adjusted direction and renationalized some strategic enterprises. The system completed morphological adjustment at the boundary and continued to operate.

### Objection 3: Node Exit

**Objection:** This paper implicitly assumes that marginal nodes will continue to participate in exchange until the system reaches zeroing. But in reality, marginal nodes may exit the system due to unemployment, illness, aging, technological substitution, and other reasons. When exits reach a certain scale, the system may lose the signal density needed to maintain stability.

**Response:** The exit of marginal nodes is a **gradual process**, not a one-time event. The system releases adjustment signals at the boundary to respond to exits. Moreover, marginal nodes "exiting" the labor market is not equivalent to "exiting" the system—they still consume, pay taxes, and occupy resources. True system collapse occurs through **large-scale, simultaneous, irreversible exits**, which are extremely rare in history.

**Case:** After Venezuela's economic collapse, over 7 million people out of a population of 27 million fled the country, and 70% of the population faced hunger. When marginal nodes exited the system on a large scale, the system indeed moved toward collapse. But even in such an extreme situation, the system continued to operate in a severely atrophied form, never completely zeroing out.

### Objection 4: Legitimacy

**Objection:** The long-term stability of a system depends not only on exchange continuity but also on the degree of recognition of the system's legitimacy by marginal nodes. When fairness rupture reaches a certain level, marginal nodes may no longer identify with the system at a cognitive level, leading to reduced trust, decreased labor input, lower willingness to consume, and ultimately weakened system efficiency.

**Response:** Legitimacy is indeed an important secondary signal, but it is **not a sufficient condition for system operation**. A system can continue to operate under declining legitimacy—as long as exchange relations continue. Marginal nodes' behavior is driven by structural constraints (survival pressure, legal enforcement, absence of alternatives), not by "identification with the system." Declining legitimacy increases the system's operating costs, but it does not immediately lead to system collapse.

**Supplementary Note:** The bearable boundary of legitimacy costs depends on the system's alternatives. When legitimacy declines to the point where marginal nodes begin to organize collective action, the system typically releases adjustment signals to reduce the appeal of organized action, thereby keeping legitimacy costs within an acceptable range.

**Case:** Nordic welfare states, while retaining the core structure of private property, compressed wealth inequality to one of the world's smallest levels through high-tax, high-welfare adjustment signals. The system maintained legitimacy through strong adjustment signals, but the core structure of private property did not change.

### Objection 5: The Buffering Effect of Adjustment Signals Is Limited

**Objection:** Adjustment signals require resources to implement. When the number of marginal groups expands and the number of people needing subsidies increases, while the resource growth of internal nodes is limited, adjustment signals at the boundary may gradually lose their buffering effect.

**Response:** This objection points to the **resource constraints** of adjustment signals, but it overestimates the rigidity of these constraints. Systems can expand adjustment space by increasing the tax contributions of internal nodes, expanding the money supply, extending retirement age, and introducing technological substitution. More importantly, the effectiveness of adjustment signals does not require covering all marginal nodes—it only requires covering **enough** nodes to prevent large-scale, synchronized exits.

**Supplementary Note:** There is a limit to how much internal nodes can bear in terms of tax adjustments. Taking China's pension system for public sector employees as an example, it already experienced a deficit of 28.08 billion yuan in 2019, requiring fiscal subsidies to fill the gap. When the tax burden on internal nodes continues to rise, their signal density may also be affected, creating new pressure at the system's boundary. Therefore, the system needs to maintain a balance between "expanding adjustment signals" and "maintaining the signal density of internal nodes."

**Case:** China's urban employees receive an average monthly pension of about 3,498 yuan, while urban and rural residents (farmers) receive only about 287 yuan per month—a huge gap. However, the system maintains operation through adjustment signals such as delayed retirement, pension system integration, and national coordination. These adjustments do not resolve the unfairness itself, but they prevent unfairness from triggering systemic collapse—the extremely low level of signal coverage is just enough to maintain the minimum participation of marginal nodes "still within the system."


## Observational Note

The analytical framework of this paper can be preliminarily observed through the following publicly available data:

- **Signal distribution differences**: OECD's *Pensions at a Glance* data shows systematic differences in pension replacement rates across countries; the gap between China's public sector and enterprise employees, and between enterprise employees and urban-rural residents, can be found in World Bank and National Bureau of Statistics data.

- **Unfairness trends**: Gini coefficient data is available through the World Bank's Poverty and Inequality Platform, covering most countries globally.

- **Existence of adjustment signals**: Trends in social security expenditure as a percentage of GDP across countries can be found in ILO and World Bank databases.

The value of the above data is not to "prove" the thesis of this paper—philosophical papers do not rely on empirical data to establish logical validity. Its value lies in showing that the structure described in this paper is not merely a logical derivation existing on paper, but an observable institutional phenomenon. Readers interested in this may verify it themselves.


## Conclusion

This paper is not a critique of private property, nor a defense of it. It is an attempt to describe how a system that distributes signals unequally can nevertheless remain stable — not because it is just, but because it is continuous. The argument does not claim that this is how things should be. It claims only that this is how they work. What to do about it belongs elsewhere.
