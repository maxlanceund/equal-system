# On the Limits of Meaning: A Verificationist Framework

**Author**: Max Lance Und  
**Status**: Working Paper  
**Date**: 2026

---

## Abstract

This paper proposes a *Limit Theory of Meaning*, according to which meaning is a signal generated by a finite system within a finite experiential interval. Outside that interval, meaning is undefined (not zero, but no longer defined). The theory is built upon a verificationist criterion: any claim that extends meaning beyond the interval relies on unverifiable premises and must be excluded from operational discourse. I trace the intellectual lineage from Wittgenstein's *Tractatus* and logical positivism, show how my theory avoids both metaphysical overreach and skeptical despair, and defend it against objections concerning sacrifice, posthumous legacy, and aesthetic martyrdom. The result is a boundary-marking tool that clarifies what can be meaningfully said and what must be consigned to silence.

**Keywords**: meaning, verificationism, limit, experiential interval, closed-loop operation, Wittgenstein

---

## 1. Introduction

What does it mean for a life, a word, or an action to be *meaningful*? Traditional answers often appeal to eternal values, cosmic justice, or posthumous recognition. This paper argues that such appeals are structurally flawed: they place the "verification button" outside the finite interval in which meaning can be generated and received. Drawing on the verificationist insight that unverifiable propositions are cognitively empty, I formulate a *Limit Theory of Meaning*:

> **Meaning = ∮_Interval Closed-loop Op | Beyond = Undefined**

In plain terms: meaning arises only within a system's operational interval (typically its lifetime), through actions that complete a feedback loop (input → processing → output). Any purported meaning that extends beyond the interval is not *negated*, but simply *undefined*—like asking for the value of a function outside its domain.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 situates the theory within the historical debate on meaning and verification. Section 3 defines the core concepts. Section 4 derives the theorem and its consequences. Section 5 answers likely objections, including sacrificial acts and the case of Yukio Mishima. Section 6 concludes with the practical import of the theory for ethics, religion, and personal life.

## 2. Historical Background: The Verificationist Tradition

The idea that meaning is tied to verifiability has a rich history. In the *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus*, Wittgenstein (1922) famously declared: "Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent." He argued that propositions only make sense if they picture possible facts; ethical, aesthetic, and religious statements, lacking factual content, fall outside the bounds of sense. The logical positivists (Carnap 1928; Ayer 1936) radicalised this into the *verification principle*: a statement is cognitively meaningful only if it is empirically verifiable (or at least confirmable).

However, the principle faced notorious difficulties: it was not itself empirically verifiable, and it seemed to disqualify scientific laws and historical claims. Later philosophers (Quine 1951; Putnam 1981) abandoned the strict criterion, but the core intuition—that meaning requires some connection to possible experience—survives in various forms (e.g., Dummett's verificationist theory of meaning, 1976).

My Limit Theory does not revive logical positivism in its original form. Instead, I adopt a *system-relative* verificationism: meaning is defined relative to a particular system (a conscious agent) and its finite operational interval. This avoids the self-refutation problem, because the criterion itself is a *definition* of how meaning is attributed within a system, not a universal empirical claim. It also respects the Wittgensteinian insight that what lies beyond the boundary must not be spoken of as if it had meaning.

## 3. Core Concepts

### 3.1 Experiential Interval (Iₑ)

The time span during which a system (e.g., a human subject) actively receives feedback signals and generates a sense of orientation. It begins when the system initiates operation and ends when it ceases signal processing (typically at death, or at the loss of consciousness).

### 3.2 Causal Trace

Observable changes in the external world caused by the system's operations. Traces can continue after the interval ends (e.g., a book written, a child saved), but they are mere physical continuations, not meaning-bearing signals for the original subject, because no feedback loop operates.

### 3.3 Meaning (M)

A signal generated within the interval that orients the system's operations—encompassing value judgments, emotional assignments, and cognitive directions. Meaning is *defined* only when the system completes a closed loop: it acts, receives feedback, and adjusts accordingly.

### 3.4 Zeroing

At the boundary of the interval, meaning ceases to be defined. This is not a negation (as if meaning became "nothing"), but a termination of the function's domain—like the value of a mathematical function outside its specified range.

## 4. The Limit Theorem and Its Derivation

**Theorem**: For any finite system S with experiential interval [0, T], the meaning function M(t) is defined only for t ∈ [0, T]. For t > T, M(t) is undefined.

**Premises**:

1. **System finitude**: Meaning depends on a system capable of receiving and processing signals. When the system ceases, meaning loses its carrier.
2. **Conditional dependence**: Meaning is not an inherent constant; it varies with the system's state, external conditions, and time.
3. **Verifiability exclusion**: Any claim that extends M(t) beyond T rests on an unverifiable premise (e.g., an afterlife, a historical accounting system), and thus cannot be part of an operational framework.

**Proof sketch**:  
If M(t) were defined for t > T, there would have to be some mechanism that continues to register, store, or update meaning without the original system. Such a mechanism would require an additional premise—e.g., a cosmic ledger, divine judgment, or historical progress—that is not observable within [0, T]. By premise 3, such premises are excluded from the definition. Therefore, M(t) is undefined outside the interval.

This theorem does not deny that causal traces persist; it only denies that those traces carry *meaning* for the subject who generated them, because the subject no longer exists to receive feedback.

## 5. Objections and Replies

### Objection 1: Sacrificial Acts

A mother sacrifices her life to save her child. Her action seems meaningful precisely because it aims at an outcome (the child's survival) beyond her own lifespan. Does the Limit Theory render such an act meaningless?

**Reply**: The objection conflates the *decision signal* within the interval with the *causal trace* outside it. At the moment of sacrifice, the mother completed a closed loop: she perceived a threat, processed the value of her child's life, and executed the action. The meaning was assigned *at that moment*—she received the internal feedback of having acted according to her value. The child's survival is a causal trace, not a cross-boundary holding of meaning. The theory does not devalue the act; it only locates its meaning firmly within the interval where it was generated.

### Objection 2: The Mishima Case

Yukio Mishima's suicide was intended to prove the value of a certain aesthetic ideal, expecting posthumous recognition. If his meaning depended on that recognition, then under the Limit Theory, his operation failed because the verification button lay outside his interval (he was mocked at his speech, and pain overwhelmed his aesthetic sense). Is this a fair assessment?

**Reply**: Yes. The tragedy of Mishima, from this perspective, lies precisely in placing the validation of his operation outside the boundary, where no signal could return. An effective meaning operation must complete its feedback loop *within* the experiential interval. This does not judge Mishima's act morally; it only points out a structural error if he expected a meaningful signal from beyond.

### Objection 3: Religious and Metaphysical Frameworks

Religion, karma, or historical progress offer meaning that transcends individual intervals. Are they all to be dismissed?

**Reply**: The Limit Theory does not deny the *subjective utility* of such frameworks within the interval—they can provide orientation and comfort. However, it denies their *cognitive validity* as extensions of meaning beyond the boundary. They are unfalsifiable promises, and as such, they fall outside the domain of meaningful discourse as defined here. One can still adhere to them as personal narratives, but they cannot be grounded in the operational logic of meaning.

## 6. Practical Applications

The theory offers clear guidelines for individual life:

- **Ethics**: Moral value is not deferred to an afterlife or historical judgement; it is enacted within the interval. Actions are meaningful if they complete a loop of intention, execution, and internal validation.
- **Death**: Death is the termination of the domain, not a negation of what was done. The integral generated within the interval is already complete; it does not require external confirmation.
- **Religion**: Faith can be a source of orientation within the interval, but its cross-boundary promises are cognitively empty. The theory neither endorses nor opposes religious practice; it only clarifies its logical status.

The theory is not a doctrine of despair. On the contrary, by removing the demand for eternity, it frees meaning to be fully realised in finite acts. A meal, a conversation, or a piece of music can be perfectly meaningful without needing to last forever.

---

## 7. Conclusion

The Limit Theory of Meaning is a boundary-marking tool, not a metaphysical system. It does not claim to discover a hidden truth about the universe; it merely proposes a definition of meaning that is internally consistent, empirically grounded in the system's operational interval, and immune to unverifiable premises. It stands in the lineage of Wittgenstein's silence and Russell's logical clarity, but it advances beyond them by formalising the boundary and offering a practical criterion for distinguishing meaningful discourse from mere speculation.

What can be said, has been said here. The rest is consigned to silence.
