# On Death and Life

I discuss the philosophical question of what death itself is.

From birth to death, human life has a beginning and an end. In reality, this is a process of dying no different from that of other animals and plants.

Human death is not special — but what is special is human thought, which has overturned the traditional notion of death as a mere termination. Generations of human thinking, philosophical works, have been continuously preserved. Writing and language have prevented death from ending history; instead, they accumulate across generations.

Since Socrates questioned, and then drank the hemlock, he confirmed that rational thinking itself can overcome bodily death.

Since Jesus was crucified, he confirmed that life may be surrendered, yet eternity endures.

In this process, life itself has also undergone transformation. Humans are not alone, for death is not an ending, but a blurring of meaning. Yet our lives also depend upon the confirmation — or love — of the Creator.

Traditional science holds that death is the end of human life. This cannot be refuted.

Philosophy does not refute this empirically verifiable fact.

Yet not everything in philosophy is impossible. When you seriously contemplate death itself, death itself has already died. If death is a determinate end, the limit of all life's meaning, where everything comes to a halt — yet it comes as a gift from the Confirmer at the unknowable boundary, and is by no means an end.

Since life has begun, you must understand: you did not voluntarily arrive in this world, nor does death owe any consolation for your life's suffering. This is irrefutable.

Thus it is irrefutable: that which gave birth to you is not your beginning. That which will die for you is not your end.

Life itself is precisely this thing that has no absolute beginning and no absolute end. If you believe your life began on a certain birth date, that itself is a mistaken cognition. Likewise, if you believe death is an ending on a certain date, that too is a mistaken cognition. Simply because life, from start to finish, does not originate from you.

Since life does not belong to you, you need not violate your free consciousness for its sake.

So what is life? It is a swimming pool ticket that you feel belongs to you, yet you have never possessed it. The ticket is issued to you; from beginning to end it has never been in your grasp, nor do you need to worry about any lack.

Philosophically, this is called the absence of meaning, called the unverifiability of the Confirmer, called the question of whether the Signer is present, and whether the signature itself can be recognized.

Only truth can confirm truth.

Naturally, only that which possesses life and death can confirm life and death.

Life is thus. Death is thus.
—— Written on July 19, 2026

*This essay is an existential extension of the "Meaning Limit Theory" applied to the question of death and life.*
