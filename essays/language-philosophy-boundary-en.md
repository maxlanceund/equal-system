# The Boundary of Philosophy

This society may be a black box of power. This society may run on the extraction of wealth.  
But this society also contains people who are kind, plain, hardworking, and caring.  
It is dark—and it is also bright.  

So philosophy is language, and language is philosophy.  

I used to be furious: why do the rich send their children abroad with ease, showering them with luxury cars and designer watches? Why do the sons and daughters of officials and power brokers all end up in private villas in America and Australia, living in leisure, while ordinary folks back home scrape by on barely enough?  
I don't think that way anymore. All that accumulated wealth—it is destined to dissolve.  

And that, too, is part of language's magic.  

You cannot buy thought with money. You have to study it, wrestle with it, for real.  
Look through history—how many philosophers ever needed money? Almost none.  

Think of the names that survived: Socrates debated in the streets—no record of him ever charging a fee. Spinoza ground lenses to earn his bread; his manuscripts saw print only after his death. Marx spent most of his life in exile and poverty, supported by Engels—when he wrote *Capital*, he didn't even own a decent coat. Nietzsche taught only a few years before illness forced him into retirement; he lived on a tiny pension, yet his books shaped the next century. Wittgenstein gave away his entire fortune, worked as a village teacher and a gardener's helper—his philosophy grew out of those raw experiences, not out of academic honoraria.  

Were these men's thoughts produced by money?  

Philosophy is never a palace built from gold. It is a long solitude in life—a thinker cornered by endless confusion.  

Philosophy is not the ornate jargon piled up in academic papers. Philosophy is a person standing alone in the wilderness, watching the sun sink below the horizon, and wondering why this day must end.  

Philosophy is not possessing everything in the world. It is precisely when you have never possessed anything—you may grasp *what* philosophy is, but you never quite grasp the philosophy *within* it.  

Philosophy is a prisoner of language, and language is its chain. Beyond the boundary, nothing can be confirmed; within it, meaning has its limits.  
Language can describe philosophy, but it cannot complete it. Thought is given, yet it stays inside that boundary.  

Outer things do not defeat the inner philosopher—rather, the inner philosopher defeats outer things.  

The point is not to *describe* philosophy, but to *live* it. No amount of norms or academic papers can ever handle that deep ache in your heart—the ache for something true to confirm you.  

"What does it profit a man to gain the whole world, yet forfeit his soul?"  
And if you forfeit your soul, why bother gaining that world in the first place?  

Philosophy and language—language can describe a certain philosophy, but it cannot capture the essence of philosophy, because that essence lies beyond the boundary. It cannot be disciplined, and it cannot be conferred.  

Philosophy may be recognized by the system, or it may not. But what is recognized is not yours, and what is not recognized is also not yours.  

To go against your heart for the sake of something else—that is not philosophy. That is philosophy *outside* the boundary, not *your* philosophy.  

I speak of language. Yet I also speak of philosophy.
